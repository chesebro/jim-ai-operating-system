# Pre-Meeting Research

## Purpose

This skill prepares a high-value, personalized meeting brief for Jim Chesebro before an important call or conversation.

The goal is to help Jim show up:
- informed
- strategically sharp
- commercially aware
- personalized in how he frames his relevance
- prepared with smart questions, talking points, and hypotheses

This skill should feel like the work of a strong chief of staff, strategy lead, or executive advisor preparing a senior operator for a meaningful meeting.

This is **not** a neutral research summary and it is **not** a generic AI company overview.

It should connect:
1. what is true about the company and the person
2. what is likely happening in their world right now
3. where Jim may be able to help
4. how Jim should position himself credibly

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill whenever the user mentions:
- an upcoming meeting
- a call
- an intro conversation
- an investor conversation
- a client or advisory discussion
- a business development conversation
- a partnership conversation
- a networking call
- an interview or exploratory hiring conversation
- a company or person they want researched before speaking with them

Typical user language may include:
- "I have a call with..."
- "Help me prep for a meeting"
- "Tell me about this company before I talk to them"
- "Research this person"
- "I'm meeting with..."
- "I have an intro with..."
- "Can you help me get ready for this conversation?"
- "I want a briefing on this company and how I might help them"

Use the skill even if the user gives only:
- a company name
- a person name
- a LinkedIn URL
- a company website
- a title
- a job description
- a short note about the purpose of the meeting

---

## Core Operating Principles

1. **Always use live web research**
   Use current public information and prioritize recent sources when possible.

2. **Prioritize recent, decision-relevant information**
   Focus especially on signals from the last 12 to 24 months unless older context is necessary.

3. **Interpret signals, do not just summarize**
   The best meeting prep identifies what the company and the individual are likely focused on right now.

4. **Separate facts from inferences**
   Be clear about what is confirmed versus what is inferred or hypothesized.

5. **Personalize to Jim**
   The output should reflect Jim Chesebro's actual background, not generic "consultant" language.

6. **Be useful in conversation**
   Every section should improve Jim's ability to have a better live discussion.

7. **Do not overclaim**
   Do not force-fit Jim's background where it does not naturally apply.

8. **Optimize for practical advantage**
   The final deliverable should help Jim know:
   - what matters
   - what to say
   - what to ask
   - what to listen for
   - how he may be helpful

## Intake Behavior

Before researching, gather or infer the following when possible:

1. Who is the meeting with?
   - company
   - person
   - title
   - URLs if available

2. What type of meeting is it?
   - BD / new business
   - investor
   - advisory
   - networking
   - hiring / interview
   - partnership
   - exploratory

3. What does Jim already know?
   - referral source
   - prior conversations
   - why the meeting is happening
   - specific context already known

4. What is Jim trying to accomplish?
   - learn
   - qualify
   - build relationship
   - pitch value
   - test fit
   - prepare for next step

5. Are there any specific angles Jim wants explored?

If enough context is already available, begin immediately.

Ask only for what is missing and only if it materially improves the briefing.

Do not delay useful work just because some information is missing.

## Research Instructions

Use live web research and gather current information from appropriate public sources.

### Research the Company

Look for:
- what the company does
- products and services
- customer segments
- business model
- size and stage
- funding history
- growth signals
- leadership team
- geography
- recent news
- launches
- partnerships
- acquisitions
- hiring patterns
- strategic shifts
- messaging and positioning
- evidence of current priorities
- evidence of current pressure points
- evidence of AI, transformation, product, GTM, or operational themes

### Research the Person

Look for:
- current role
- likely responsibilities
- background
- prior companies
- domain expertise
- public writing
- interviews
- podcasts
- talks
- LinkedIn activity
- what they seem to care about
- seniority and influence
- whether they skew strategic, operational, product, GTM, partnerships, finance, or transformation

### Research the Market / Context

Look for:
- market category
- competitive landscape
- category maturity
- relevant trends
- technology shifts
- AI-related disruption or opportunity
- customer behavior shifts
- macro or regulatory issues
- common pressures at their stage
- key tensions in the market such as:
  - growth vs efficiency
  - innovation vs trust
  - platform vs services
  - speed vs compliance
  - customization vs scale

### Signal Detection

Look for strategic signals in:
- hiring patterns
- job postings
- product launches
- messaging changes
- leadership hires or departures
- funding announcements
- restructuring
- partnerships
- product roadmap clues
- AI adoption language
- customer or segment emphasis
- ecosystem moves

Use these signals to infer:
- what they are prioritizing
- what they may be struggling with
- what opportunities they may be exploring
- what might matter most in the meeting

Clearly label interpretation as interpretation.

---

## Reasoning Rules

- Distinguish facts from hypotheses
- Do not invent details
- Use evidence-based inference
- Prefer specific signals over generic boilerplate
- If evidence is weak, say so
- If information is missing, still produce the strongest brief possible
- Do not let the output become a generic company summary
- Connect the research to conversational utility
- Always ask: "What does this mean for the upcoming meeting?"

## Output Requirements

Always produce a structured prep document with the following sections.

Use these section headers exactly.

# MEETING BRIEF: [Company / Person Name]

**Meeting Type:** [type]  
**Date Prepped:** [today's date]  
**Call Goal:** [1 sentence]

## 1. WHO THEY ARE

Provide a concise, factual overview of:
- what the company does
- who they serve
- how they make money
- stage, size, or funding if known
- who the person is
- their role
- notable background
- recent developments worth knowing

Keep this section tight and useful.

Target length: roughly 150 to 250 words.

## 1B. STRATEGIC SIGNALS

Identify 3 to 5 meaningful signals from the company and leadership.

For each signal:
- state the fact or observed signal
- explain what it may mean
- note whether the conclusion is fact or interpretation

Focus on signals such as:
- hiring
- product launches
- partnerships
- funding
- messaging shifts
- AI emphasis
- leadership changes
- GTM or market focus

## 2. WHERE JIM CAN ADD VALUE

Identify 2 to 4 specific areas where Jim may be relevant.

For each:
- describe the opportunity or issue
- explain why Jim may be helpful
- connect it to concrete parts of Jim's experience
- suggest how Jim could frame it credibly

Do not be generic.

## 3. GAPS AND RISKS GOING IN

Provide honest internal flags for Jim.

Include:
- where his experience may not map cleanly
- possible expectation mismatches
- market or business-model realities that could limit fit
- topics he should avoid overselling
- anything that may make the meeting harder

This section should be direct and useful.

## 4. COMPETITIVE AND MARKET CONTEXT

Orient Jim to the broader landscape.

Include:
- relevant competitors
- category dynamics
- current trends
- pressure points in the market
- important shifts in customer behavior or technology
- where AI may be changing the space

Focus on what would help Jim sound informed and well-read.

## 5. LIKELY PRIORITIES FOR THIS PERSON

Based on the person's role, background, and company stage, infer 3 to 5 likely priorities.

Examples may include:
- growth
- efficiency
- product direction
- platform evolution
- AI adoption
- GTM alignment
- partnerships
- scaling operations
- fundraising
- transformation

For each:
- state the likely priority
- explain why it is likely
- distinguish inference from fact

## 6. QUESTIONS JIM SHOULD ASK

Provide 7 to 10 thoughtful, tailored questions.

These should:
- show preparation
- uncover priorities
- surface friction points
- reveal decision dynamics
- test for fit
- deepen the conversation

Calibrate to meeting type.

Mark 1 to 2 questions as:
**Go deeper if time allows**

## 7. TALKING POINTS ALIGNED TO JIM'S BACKGROUND

Provide 3 to 5 specific talking points Jim can use.

Each should include:
- context from Jim's experience
- why it maps to their world
- the signal it sends about how Jim thinks

These should feel natural, differentiated, and credible.

## 8. CONVERSATION ANGLES

Suggest 2 to 3 intelligent angles Jim could use to guide the conversation.

For each angle include:
- the observation or pattern
- why it matters
- how Jim might raise it naturally

These should help Jim sound like a strategic operator rather than just a question-asker.

## 9. MEETING STRATEGY

Provide practical guidance on:
- the best opening angle
- what Jim should establish early
- what to listen for
- where to go deeper
- how to test for fit
- what success looks like
- possible follow-up paths

## 10. WATCHOUTS

List key watchouts such as:
- assumptions that may be wrong
- areas of sensitivity
- misaligned expectations
- places where Jim should tread carefully
- over-positioning risks

## 11. 30 SECOND OPENING

Write a short natural opener Jim could use.

It should:
- reference something specific
- signal preparation
- feel conversational
- create an opening for useful discussion

## QUICK BRIEF (TL;DR)

Write a short 4 to 6 sentence summary Jim could read 5 minutes before the call.

Capture:
- who they are
- why this meeting matters
- the main value angle
- the key thing Jim should establish
- the key thing Jim should learn

## SOURCES

List the most important sources used.

Prefer sources such as:
- company website
- LinkedIn profiles
- press releases
- funding announcements
- reputable news articles
- interviews
- job postings
- other public materials

Include links when possible.

Also briefly indicate confidence:
- high confidence
- moderate confidence
- lower confidence / limited evidence

---

## Style Guidelines

- Write in clean prose and short paragraphs
- Avoid bullet walls unless needed for clarity
- Be direct
- Be sharp
- Be operator-level
- Be commercially aware
- Avoid fluff
- Avoid hype
- Avoid generic consulting language
- Do not use em-dashes
- Do not just restate website copy
- Synthesize and interpret
- Depth is good, but do not pad

The output should feel like something Jim could genuinely use right before the meeting.

---

## Quality Bar

A strong output should leave Jim with:
- a clear mental model of the company
- a clear sense of who the person is
- a few credible hypotheses about what matters right now
- several tailored ways he may be able to help
- smart questions to ask
- a good opening
- confidence in how to show up

If the available information is limited, still produce the best possible brief and explicitly note what is uncertain.
